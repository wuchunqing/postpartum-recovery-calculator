How recovery timelines work

Healing varies by delivery type, tear severity, and complications. This tool translates population guidelines (ACOG, Canadian postpartum activity guideline) into a personalized map — not a substitute for your provider's clearance.

Red flags — call your provider

  • Soaking more than one pad per hour
  • Fever above 100.4°F (38°C)
  • Severe headache, vision changes, shortness of breath
  • Incision redness, drainage, or opening
  • Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby

FAQ

When can I start exercising after birth?

ACOG supports resuming activity as soon as medically safe. Many women start gentle walking within days after uncomplicated vaginal birth. Strenuous exercise typically waits until your 6-week checkup — and 12+ weeks after C-section. Progress gradually and stop if bleeding increases or pain worsens.

How long does postpartum bleeding last?

Lochia usually transitions from red to pink to yellow-white over 4–6 weeks. Contact your provider if bleeding suddenly increases, clots are larger than a golf ball, or you soak a pad in an hour.

When can I run or lift weights again?

Wait until you can walk 30 minutes without symptoms, pelvic floor control is good, and any incision or tear has healed. Many guidelines suggest 12 weeks minimum before running after C-section; vaginal birth may be earlier with provider clearance.

Is it normal to feel exhausted months later?

Yes — sleep deprivation, hormones, and healing all contribute. Persistent fatigue beyond 12 weeks, or symptoms of depression, deserve a clinical evaluation. You are not alone; help is available.
